Product Design UX Strategy Westfield Health

Creating a market-first broker quotation tool

Various screens from the Westfield Health broker tool. All design by me.

Role
Lead UX Designer (one-person team)
Team
Cross-functional product team via Accenture
Duration
Mar 2023 – Sept 2025
Impact
First-to-market platform; design practice and system from zero

The situation

Westfield Health, a not-for-profit health insurer, was watching its core demographic age out. The majority of its customers were elderly individuals, and to secure the longevity of the business it needed to attract new corporate-sector customers. The route in was their broker channel — independent advisors who quote and sell health insurance to companies — but the existing quotation process was manual, time-consuming, costly, and inefficient.

I joined as the only designer on a cross-functional product team via Accenture. The brief: build a self-serve broker quotation platform from scratch. Reduce manual effort. Save money. Drive new business. And do it ahead of competitors, because being first-to-market in this corner of UK private health was a real and time-sensitive opportunity.

The broker landing experience. Design by me; cross-functional team of business analysts, developers, content designers and subject-matter experts.

The constraint that shaped everything

Being first-to-market created a challenge. Because of confidentiality, we couldn’t talk directly to brokers — our users — to avoid leaking information to competitors. This meant we had to find different ways to research and test our ideas.

This is the part of the project I’d most want to talk about in an interview. Standard UX practice says you talk to your users. We couldn’t. The work would have leaked into competitor hands within a week, and the commercial advantage we were building would have evaporated. So we had to do user-centred design without direct user access, and we had to do it credibly enough that stakeholders trusted the output to ship into production.

Working without users

The team adopted a research approach built on what we did have: subject-matter experts inside Westfield who had spent careers selling to brokers, business analysts who had mapped the existing process in detail, and publicly-available material on how the broker market operates. I led interviewing of internal stakeholders, developed pseudo-personas grounded in their accumulated knowledge, and documented every assumption explicitly so we could revisit and revise as evidence emerged.

Research artefacts: pseudo-personas built from internal expert interviews, and the assumption log we ran throughout the project.

I also democratised design across the team. With no users to talk to, the next-best thing was to make our thinking maximally visible to everyone with relevant knowledge. I shared artefacts and documentation for ongoing feedback, involved experts and stakeholders in our pseudo-usability and ideation sessions, and ran frequent internal testing. The discipline I built into the practice — best-practice patterns, frequent reviews, adopting proven components — meant we were leaning on the wider design community’s collective knowledge wherever our direct evidence was thin.

Building the design system in parallel

As the project began to scale rapidly, the need for a design system became apparent quickly. So I built one. I leveraged Figma’s design system tooling — using variables for global tokens like spacing, typography, and colour — to enable features like auto responsiveness and improve polish. The early use of atomic patterns led to a mature system with reusable templates, cutting design time, ensuring consistency, and freeing the team to focus on innovation.

A progressive design system. Variables for tokens, atomic components, and the screens those components built — all design by me.

Where the project landed

The broker tool launched into beta with a select group of trusted brokers — validating the experience with real users at the point we could finally bring them in, and allowing us to pivot if needed. By that stage the constraint that had defined the project was no longer a blocker; it had forced a more rigorous design practice than we’d otherwise have built, and that practice persisted past launch.

What I took from this

Two things. First: user-centred design isn’t the same thing as user-access design. When you can’t talk to the people you’re designing for, you have to find every other source of evidence and triangulate hard. The work is harder, the confidence is lower, but the discipline is the same. Second: a design system built reactively, in parallel with shipping product, is a more valuable artefact than one built upfront in isolation. It earns its keep by solving real problems as they come up.

This was a one-person design team operating inside a cross-functional product squad. The cross-functional team — business analysts, developers, subject-matter experts, content designers — did the heavy lifting on everything that wasn’t design. Credit where it’s due.
