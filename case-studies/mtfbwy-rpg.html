Why this is here

This is a personal project, not a client one. It’s on the portfolio because building a tabletop role-playing game from scratch turns out to be a remarkably good test of every skill I claim to have in the day job: systems thinking, iterative design, information architecture, accessibility, and the discipline of testing with real users.

What it is

MTFBWY is a Star Wars-inspired TTRPG I’ve been designing in my spare time. The scope spans physical and digital: the rulebook, character sheets, GM tools, and a virtual tabletop platform I’m prototyping with developer friends so groups can play remotely. Everything has to work for the player at the table, which means the rules system, the UI of the character sheet, and the digital tooling are all parts of one experience.

The virtual tabletop sign-up experience, character sheet PDF, and rulebook layout. All design by me.

How I work on it

Weekly playtests with real players. Iteration on rules, UI, and storytelling flow based on what actually happens at the table — not what I think should happen. The mechanics, the visual language, and the digital tooling have all been substantially rewritten more than once because real play surfaced friction the design didn’t anticipate. The discipline is identical to product UX: ship something, watch real users break it, fix what matters, ignore what doesn’t.

Why I’m showing it

Two reasons. First: it’s the clearest demonstration I have of systems thinking. Designing a game is designing rules, then designing the interfaces to those rules, then designing how players learn the rules from the interfaces — all the way down. Second: it’s a reminder that the best designers are also users of the things they make. Sitting at a table watching someone struggle to find the right rule on the right page is the most direct UX feedback I get all week.
